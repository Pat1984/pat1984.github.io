##visual significance

Colors can influence composition and also reinforce hierarchy, structure, and relationships. The use of these colors requires designers to possess another form of visual literacy. This involves the cultural domain where designers need to observe and respond.

  • The meaning of color is decoded by specific audiences based on factors such as context, education, and age. In many parts of the world, color combined with shape is used to signify meaning. The road traffic sign system is a good example.
  • The use of text refers not only to the printed words on paper but also to other activities related to cultural production, such as forms of visual and auditory communication. French philosopher Roland Barthes began challenging the traditional notion of the author's central and controlling influence on the meaning of texts in the late 1960s and early 1970s.
  • Barthes argues in his essays "The Death of the Author" and "From Work to Text" that while the author's influence on the text can be traced, the meaning of the text itself does not inherently exist within the object, nor can it be reduced to the author's intentions. The unity of a text lies not in its origin but in its destination.
  • Visual literacy includes not only an understanding of the primary meaning of things but also the interpretation and indication of their secondary meanings. A comprehensive approach to visual literacy can be seen as part of visual communication. Effective design is created.
  • Semiotics or semantics can be considered a comprehensive approach to visual communication. This effective design includes not only an understanding of the primary meanings but also the interpretation and indication of secondary meanings.
  • Complex social issues are not resolved merely by doing things; things must be done well. This requires effort, intelligence, cultural and ethical sensitivity, resources, and institutional support. A design response to a social issue should not be seen as merely creating some posters and flyers, telling people what to do and what not to do.

Connotation and Denotation

The term denotation describes the original literal meaning of an image or a piece of communication, usually related to a specific target audience or reader group. Like a name or noun, it describes what an object is, not what it represents. Understanding this aspect of reading (decoding) and writing or making (encoding) meaning in messages is crucial to all forms of communication.

Graphic designers need to be aware of the use of specific visual symbols and icons and their common meanings within the target group. This is especially true in information design and other areas of graphic design that attempt to reach a wide audience, relying heavily on the denotation of specific meanings in visual forms to make the intended message clear and understandable. Here, the specific material quality of the visual form itself is also important: for example, it may be handwritten, typeset, drawn, photographed, or displayed on a screen, each of which affects how the message is interpreted.

Connotation refers to the range of secondary meanings of a form of communication (such as text; written, spoken, or visual), whether intended or unintentional: a series of meanings and interpretations of an object or thing, its qualities and impressions in different cultural, social, or personal contexts.

Analysis and Proposition

Research methods can be defined as the ways to solve design problems or investigate work environments. This chapter focuses on the thematic approach to problem-solving and constructing a rational and logical system of design thinking. By enhancing the understanding of existing visual conventions and developing and applying a personal visual vocabulary, designers can make more effective use of their perception and discovery, and work practically and creatively within a broader cultural context. A systematic research method encourages designers to document, record, and evaluate visual and verbal structures, languages, and identities in a wider context, and then apply these structures, languages, and identities to develop personal and critical viewpoints. Adopting a rigorous approach to solving specific brief requirements and setting a series of boundaries for broader research allows designers to focus on a project and define the problems to be solved, or a series of problems. By breaking down a project into a set of intentions, each with defined parameters and predetermined background knowledge or experience, the designer makes the task easier to achieve and the goals of each stage more explicit. This chapter will detail each domain, demonstrating the development process of strategic design methods related to the brief. It includes examples of work that elucidate key concepts in professional and academic fields.
